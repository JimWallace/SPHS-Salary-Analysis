\section*{Executive Summary}

Faculty affiliated with the Master of Health Informatics (MHI) have raised concerns that the annual performance review process may disadvantage interdisciplinary research relative to traditional public health domains. This report evaluates whether such disadvantage is observable in publicly available salary data from \DataMinYear--\DataMaxYear  (41 faculty; 369 person-years). The analysis asks whether MHI faculty earn less and whether they receive smaller annual salary increases. Pooled comparisons indicate that MHI faculty salaries are approximately \$33{,}541 lower at the centered year (95\% CI [\$-53{,}957, \$-13{,}125]); although this estimate does not fully adjust for rank or promotion timing, it reflects a substantial observed salary gap. When faculty are compared at similar career stages, entry-cohort models estimate annual growth gaps of roughly \$1{,}565 to \$2{,}287 less per year for MHI faculty in primary specifications, and \$3{,}456 less per year in the matched early-career average-slope check. Exact permutation inference gives \(p=0.532\) for pooled entry-cohort FE, \(p=0.067\) for within-cohort FE, and \(p=0.008\) for matched-entry FE. Because salary increments are the observable output of the APR process, these directionally and often statistically negative growth differences support continued institutional scrutiny while acknowledging the limits of publicly available data.



\section*{Introduction}

Evaluating multidisciplinary scholarship within traditionally structured academic units is widely recognized as challenging. Different disciplines signal quality, contribution, and impact in different ways: authorship conventions vary, publication venues carry different meanings, and applied or community-engaged work may prioritize policy briefs, technical reports, or knowledge mobilization over journal articles. University of Waterloo Policy 77 establishes that performance reviews must be fair, consistent, and grounded in transparent criteria across academic units.\footnote{\url{https://uwaterloo.ca/secretariat/policies-procedures-guidelines/policy-77}} The Memorandum of Agreement between the University and FAUW similarly emphasizes equity, procedural integrity, and defined processes linking performance review to salary decisions.\footnote{\url{https://uwaterloo.ca/secretariat/memorandum-agreement-uw-fauw}} At the School level, the SPHS performance review guidelines explicitly recognize multidisciplinarity and instruct that scholarly outputs be interpreted in light of disciplinary traditions, with quality and impact assessed using multiple complementary indicators.\footnote{SPHS Faculty Performance Review Guidelines, 2025 update (internal document).}

In principle, the institutional framework is designed to support equitable evaluation across disciplinary boundaries. Policy 77 requires that performance assessment be consistent with University policy and collective agreements. The Memorandum of Agreement (Article 13) governs the annual or biennial review process and the distribution of selective salary increments. The SPHS guidelines further state that indicators must have face validity, that benchmarking should occur against comparable faculty, and that multidisciplinary traditions should be respected when interpreting research outputs. Taken together, these documents articulate a clear institutional commitment: interdisciplinary faculty should be evaluated using standards appropriate to their domain, not disadvantaged by disciplinary mismatch.

This report examines whether that commitment is reflected in observable outcomes. Using publicly available salary disclosures from \DataMinYear--\DataMaxYear,\footnote{Salary disclosure archives available at \url{https://uwaterloo.ca/about/accountability/salary-disclosure}} we analyze salary levels and salary growth for MHI and non-MHI faculty within SPHS. Salary is an imperfect proxy for institutional valuation, but it is the only fully public and longitudinal indicator available. Since the creation of the MHI program in 2013, estimated growth differences are not uniform across pooled and fixed-effects specifications. We outline concrete next steps to ensure that multidisciplinary scholarship is evaluated in a manner fully consistent with University policy, collective agreements, and the School’s stated commitments.

\section*{Methods}

This analysis uses publicly available University of Waterloo salary disclosure records for the years \DataMinYear--\DataMaxYear.\footnote{\url{https://uwaterloo.ca/about/accountability/salary-disclosure}} These disclosures report annual salaries for employees above the provincial reporting threshold. Faculty names were extracted for each year and harmonized across reporting periods to construct a longitudinal panel. Faculty affiliation with the Master of Health Informatics (MHI) was determined using the publicly available SPHS Health Informatics faculty roster.\footnote{\url{https://uwaterloo.ca/public-health-sciences/our-people/health-informatics-researchers}} No internal personnel records, APR ratings, merit scores, promotion histories, or workload assignments were used. The resulting dataset includes 41 faculty members and 369 person-year observations. The panel is unbalanced; missing years were not imputed. These records constitute the complete set of publicly observable compensation trajectories for SPHS faculty meeting provincial disclosure criteria during the study period.

The analysis addresses two questions. First, whether MHI faculty earn less overall. Salary level differences were estimated using pooled regression models adjusting for calendar year. Because rank and promotion timing are not observed in public disclosures, this comparison does not fully adjust for career stage.

Second, whether MHI faculty receive smaller annual salary increases. Salary growth differences were estimated by modelling annual changes in salary and testing whether growth rates differ by MHI status. To better account for career stage, we estimate growth differences using (i) pooled models, (ii) within-person models, and (iii) entry-aligned comparisons that match faculty hired within $\pm 1$ year. Entry-aligned models provide the closest approximation to comparisons at similar career stages.

Because public salary disclosures do not include APR ratings, promotion dates, or workload assignments, salary serves as an observable proxy for evaluation outcomes. The analysis therefore examines whether publicly observable compensation trajectories are consistent with equitable treatment across disciplinary domains.

\section{6. Results}

The results are organized around the two research questions: (1) Do MHI faculty earn less? and (2) Do MHI faculty receive smaller annual salary increases?

\subsection*{6.1 Do MHI Faculty Earn Less?}
Pooled comparisons indicate that MHI faculty salaries are approximately \(-\$33{,}541\) lower at the centered year (95\% CI: \([-\$53{,}957,\ -\$13{,}125]\)). This remains a substantial observed difference within the full population of disclosed SPHS faculty. However, public salary data do not include rank, promotion timing, or years since hire, and MHI faculty are disproportionately more recent hires. The level comparison therefore does not fully adjust for career stage and cannot by itself distinguish between disciplinary disadvantage and differences in seniority.

For this reason, the more informative question is whether salaries grow at different rates when faculty are compared at similar career stages. If growth rates differ systematically, level gaps would be expected to widen over time. Section 6.2 therefore examines whether MHI faculty receive smaller annual salary increases.


\subsection*{6.2 Do MHI Faculty Receive Smaller Annual Salary Increases?}

When growth is examined without aligning career stage, results are mixed: pooled models estimate \(+\$330\)/year (95\% CI: \([-\$1{,}454,\ +\$2{,}114]\)), while within-person models estimate \(-\$1{,}431\)/year (95\% CI: \([-\$2{,}279,\ -\$583]\)). These differences indicate sensitivity to specification when entry timing is not explicitly addressed.

When faculty are compared at similar career stages, estimates are directionally negative and larger in entry-aligned designs. Entry-cohort models estimate growth gaps of approximately \(-\$1{,}565\)/year (pooled with cohort effects) and \(-\$1{,}798\)/year (within-cohort FE), while matched-entry models (faculty hired within \(\pm 1\) year) estimate \(-\$2{,}287\)/year (95\% CI: \([-\$3{,}253,\ -\$1{,}321]\)). The matched early-career average-slope estimate is \(-\$3{,}456\)/year (95\% CI: \([-\$5{,}576,\ -\$1{,}336]\)). Exact permutation inference yields \(p=0.532\) for pooled entry-cohort FE, \(p=0.067\) for within-cohort FE, and \(p=0.008\) for matched-entry FE.

An annual difference on the order of \$1{,}500–\$3{,}000 could still compound over time into a meaningful cumulative compensation gap. Because annual salary increments reflect APR outcomes, the direction and persistence of these growth differences remain consistent with concern about differential evaluation, while statistical certainty varies by specification.







\section{10. Conclusion}

Across the full set of publicly observable SPHS salary disclosures, the evidence points to a consistent disadvantage for MHI-affiliated faculty: salaries are lower in level and, in most career-stage-aligned models, annual growth is also weaker. While this study cannot prove mechanism without internal APR and promotion records, the direction, size, and persistence of the observed gaps are substantial enough to justify immediate institutional review rather than watchful waiting. In practical terms, if annual increments differ by roughly \$1{,}500--\$3{,}000 over multiple years, cumulative compensation inequities can become large even when year-to-year changes appear modest. The strongest next step is a formal equity audit that links salary outcomes to rank, workload, and review inputs, and then uses those findings to tighten APR criteria so multidisciplinary scholarship is evaluated consistently with SPHS policy commitments.
